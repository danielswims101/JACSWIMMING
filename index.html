Energy Systems

AEROBIC THRESHOLD (AeT)

Training at or below 2–3 mmol/L blood lactate builds mitochondrial density, oxidative enzyme capacity, and fat oxidation — the true foundation of all swimming performance. HR cue: 26–28 on the Golf scale (beats per 10 seconds). Going faster doesn't produce better aerobic adaptations; it produces worse ones with longer recovery cost.

  • Recovery: <1.5 mmol — active rest only
  • Sub-threshold: 1.5–2.5 mmol — comfortable aerobic base
  • AeT zone: 2.5–3.0 mmol — primary training target
  • Threshold: 3.0–4.5 mmol — race-pace endurance
  • VO2 max: 4.5–8+ mmol — speed endurance work
Periodization

MESO-CYCLE STRUCTURE

Meso I (Weeks 1–4) establishes aerobic base stroke-by-stroke with progressive volume loading. Week 4 introduces threshold for the first time. Meso II stacks volume (+25%), adds cross-stroke sets, and layers threshold work on top of an established aerobic foundation.

  • Wk 1: Freestyle AeT base — 20×50 benchmark
  • Wk 2: Backstroke + FR crossover — 5×100 BK
  • Wk 3: Breaststroke + Butterfly integration
  • Wk 4: First threshold exposure — 6×400 FR
  • Meso 2 Wk 5–8: All strokes + threshold volume climbs

Olympic-Level Execution
TECHNIQUE LIBRARY

Exhaustive breakdowns of all four strokes, underwater phases, and race turns with coaching cues used at Olympic-level practice.

ENTRY & CATCH — The Power Position

▾

Elite freestyle starts before the pull. The hand enters narrow — inside the shoulder line, fingertips first at a slight downward angle, zero splash. The arm extends fully forward before engaging the catch, allowing the shoulder to rotate fully over the centerline. Caeleb Dressel enters at 11 and 1 o'clock — never wider. The "reach" phase is not passive: you're extending from the hip girdle and lats, loading the catch before the hand moves backward at all.

🎯 Cue: "Reach around a barrel" — your high elbow sets before the hand ever pulls back. Press the elbow out, not down. The forearm is a paddle; the elbow is the fulcrum.
Entry angle

10–15° below horizontal — never flat or over-rotated. Splash = wasted energy.

Elbow position

Elbow stays above wrist throughout entire catch. High elbow = large propulsive surface.

Catch depth

Wrist bends to vertical at 30–40cm depth before the pull initiates.

Elite benchmark

Catch angle of wrist to forearm: 90° or less at maximum catch depth.

ROTATION & BODY POSITION

▾

Body rotation in elite freestyle is driven from the hips, not the shoulders. Sprint rotation: 35–45°. Distance rotation: 25–35°. Rotation is timed with the kick — when the left hand catches, the right hip drives down. This isn't a conscious roll; it's the result of a powerful kick that torques the hips and loads the pulling arm's lat before the pull begins.

🎯 Cue: "Your hips drive your arms — not the other way around." If fatigued and losing rotation, the kick died first. Fix the kick, not the arms.
Head position

Hairline at surface, eyes at pool bottom. Never look forward — adds drag immediately.

Hip height

Hips must be near the surface. Sinking hips add exponential drag.

Shoulder axis

Shoulders rotate as a unit. Avoid "independent shoulder" wobble that wastes energy.

Foot test

Feet leave a shallow wake — not splashing sideways. Narrow is fast.

PULL PATTERN & FINISH

▾

Modern elite freestyle uses a modified straight-path pull — hand tracks relatively straight beneath body centerline. The old exaggerated S-pull is obsolete. What matters is maximum "still water" contact throughout. Elite swimmers like Sjöström accelerate from catch to finish — slowest at catch, fastest at the hip. Exiting at the belly button cuts power by ~30%.

🎯 Cue: "Paint the lane line black from wrist to thigh." The finish extends past the hip — wrist turns outward, thumb points down on exit.
Pull path

Near-straight under body centerline. Avoid wide sweeping that pushes dead water.

Acceleration

Hand speed increases from catch to finish — never decelerates mid-pull.

Exit point

Hand exits at the thigh, fingers back, wrist supinated (thumb down).

Recovery

High elbow for 200m+. Lower elbow acceptable only for pure sprinting.

GOLF SCORE — Your AeT Pacing Tool

▾

Golf Score = Split (seconds per 50m) + Stroke Count per 50. A swimmer targeting 30-stroke / 30-second 50m has a Golf score of 60. The goal: find the stroke rate + DPS combination that minimizes score, then gradually increase tempo while holding or improving it. Elite age-group targets: 55–70 depending on event and height.

🎯 Your Program: "Des 1–5 to Golf BA" — descend reps 1–5 tuning stroke rate until you find the fastest-split/lowest-count combination. That's your Golf Best Average. Hold it for reps 6–10.
Score targets

Below 55: world class. 55–62: senior national. 62–70: strong competitive.

Recording

Log split + count every rep. Weekly trends reveal real improvement.

DPS +2

Try 2 fewer strokes per length than normal. Forces technique adaptation.

HR target

AeT tech work only: HR 26–28 Golf scale. Never threshold during Golf sets.

FREESTYLE KICK — The Hidden Engine

▾

Elite sprinters derive 30–40% of propulsion from the kick. The 6-beat kick (6 kicks per full arm cycle) is standard for sprints and middle distance. The 2-beat is used by many elite distance swimmers to conserve energy. The kick originates from the hip — a knee-driven "bicycle" kick creates drag and zero propulsion. Daily ankle plantarflexion stretching is the single highest-return physical investment for kick speed.

🎯 Cue: "Kick from your hip pocket." The foot is a passive flipper — it follows the hip drive. Band pull sets in your program isolate hip drive by removing the kick entirely, then reintegrating it.

BODY POSITION & THE DUCK DRILL

▾

The "Duck" device keeps your head in correct backstroke alignment. Correct position: hips at the surface, ears submerged, eyes slightly toward your feet. Ryan Murphy's hips are so high his knees barely break the surface on kick. Any hip drop adds drag exponentially. Think of floating on the surface, not swimming through it.

🎯 Cue: "Squeeze a coin between your glutes and point your belly button at the ceiling." Hip drop is almost always a core/awareness issue, not flexibility.
Head position

No chin tuck, no looking at feet. One ear depth below surface — neutral neck.

Kick plane

Kick from hip — feet barely break the surface. Knee-driven = immediate drag.

Shoulder roll

45–55° roll each stroke. More rotation = more lat engagement = more power.

Duck tool

Real-time proprioceptive feedback on neck and head position during training.

CATCH & PULL — The Pinky Entry & 2-2-2 Drill

▾

Backstroke hand entry is pinky-first, hand rotated so the thumb points toward the centerline. Arm enters directly above the shoulder — never crossing the centerline (creates snake-body effect). Extend fully before catch begins — reach past your head, feel the lats engage, then initiate with a high elbow pressing outward. The S-curve pull path is more prominent in backstroke than freestyle.

🎯 2-2-2 Drill (your program): Two right-arm strokes → two left-arm strokes → two full strokes. Isolates the catch without tempo pressure. Listen for clean, quiet entry — noise means the hand is entering wrong.
Entry

Pinky leads, arm enters at 12–1 o'clock (shoulder-width or just inside).

Catch depth

Hand descends ~45cm before pressing outward to initiate the pull.

Pull path

S-curve: press out → sweep in → push back. All connected, no pauses.

Exit

Thumb exits at hip. Elbow bends on recovery — stays over body centerline.

BACKSTROKE KICK & TEMPO

▾

Backstroke uses a 6-beat kick — 6 kicks per stroke cycle. The kick is narrow (no wider than hip-width), fast, and driven from hip flexors. The upward kick is propulsive — toes pointed up, ankle plantarflexed. Elite BK kick tempo: 150–180 kicks/min. Your program's kick-on-back fin sets load hip flexors and build the tempo endurance needed for race pace. Head stays completely still using a ceiling tile as a fixed reference.

🎯 "Heavy Fish Kick" drill: Exaggerated undulation on your side with dolphin kick pattern. Bridges backstroke kick mechanics to the rotation rhythm needed for race-speed backstroke. One of the best BK transition drills.

THE 2K1 SCULL — Your Program's Core Drill

▾

"2k1s" (2 kicks, 1 stroke) is the foundational breaststroke drill. It forces the swimmer to feel the glide and catch every cycle by doubling the kick before each pull, slowing tempo and isolating pull initiation. Elite swimmers like Qin Haiyang use an extremely patient out-scull before the catch — hands move OUT before going down. Most swimmers rush this and collapse elbows inward too early.

🎯 Your Program: "Deep 2k1 scull / Shallow 2k1 scull / Flat rhythm" — progressively decreases scull depth across the set, transitioning from deep drill feel to race-speed flat feel. All three phases teach the same skill at different speeds.
Scull path

Hands press out, then in, then sweep back — full heart-shaped pattern.

Deep version

Hands below chest — exaggerates feel for water pressure against palm.

Flat version

Hands stay at surface plane — matches actual race technique timing.

Flat connect

Drill feel linked directly into full-speed swimming within the same rep.

BREASTSTROKE KICK — The Engine (60–70% of Propulsion)

▾

Breaststroke kick accounts for 60–70% of all propulsion. Heels draw up narrow (not wide — creates drag), feet flex outward (eversion — duck feet), then sweep out-and-around in a circular pattern, finishing with feet together. Most common flaw: bicycle kick where one foot sweeps asymmetrically. Your 10×100 BR kick descend sets build the specific endurance and feel needed for race-speed kick through the full 200m.

🎯 "BR pull make" sets: Isolate the pull using a pull buoy. If you can't feel propulsion from arms alone in breaststroke, your elbow is collapsing during the in-sweep — the single most common breaststroke arm error across all levels.

TIMING — Pull · Breathe · Kick · Glide (Sequence is Law)

▾

The pull and kick must never overlap. The rigid sequence: PULL → breathe → kick → glide. Any overlap creates opposing forces that cancel propulsion. The breath happens during the in-sweep when hands come inward and elbows tuck — the head rides the natural body lift from the pull. Adam Peaty's head barely clears the surface even in sprint 100m races. The DPS+2 / DPS+4 sets in your program systematically challenge timing by reducing stroke count.

🎯 Build-each-25 rule: +1, +2, +3, +4 effort per 25 of a 100. Each 25 builds tempo — trains timing precision under race-velocity fatigue, which is exactly where most athletes break down in the final 50m.

UNDULATION — The Wave From Ground Up

▾

Butterfly is not arm-driven. Movement initiates from the chest pressing down, which causes the hips to rise, which causes the feet to kick down. It's a wave. Michael Phelps' butterfly is described as "dolphin-like" because his undulation starts at the chest and travels through the hips with zero interruption. The 2-2-2 drill in your program teaches each phase in isolation before connecting them.

🎯 2-2-2 Drill: Two right-arm strokes → two left-arm strokes → two full butterfly. One dolphin kick per arm stroke in drill mode. Isolates the one-arm pull pattern and makes asymmetries immediately obvious before they become ingrained habits.
Wave initiation

Chest DOWN first — hips rise as a consequence, not simultaneously.

Kick timing

First kick: at entry/extension. Second kick: at the push/exit phase.

Amplitude

Hip undulation 20–30cm. Too much = drag. Too little = no propulsion.

OA fly drill

One-arm fly isolates catch while the free arm provides balance reference.

NO-SPLASH ENTRY — Wiff Drill & Paddle Progressions

▾

The "Wiff" tool (resistance-free paddle) is in your program for butterfly entry work. A big splash on hand entry creates drag and destroys feel for the catch. Elite swimmers like Sjöström enter with almost zero surface disturbance — hands slice in at 45° downward angle, thumbs slightly down, arms wider than shoulders but not dramatically.

🎯 Three-tool progression in your program: Wiff → Paddle on top → Fingertip paddle. Wiff teaches entry angle via sound. Padd on top teaches wrist plane via balance. Fingertip teaches palm sensitivity. "No splash" is the constraint across all three.

UNDERWATER DOLPHIN KICK — The 5th Stroke

▾

Underwater dolphin kick is the fastest way humans move through water. At elite levels, swimmers hold higher velocity underwater than on the surface. Your program features UW 15m on nearly every session — 15m is the legal maximum. Caeleb Dressel's kick velocity off the wall exceeds his surface sprint pace. The kick is driven from the hips — a wave from chest through hips to feet. Amplitude: 25–35cm. Elite rate: 180–210 kicks/min.

🎯 "UW 15 IM Drill @ 1:00" in your program: every rep maximize depth hold, streamline tightness, and kick tempo before breaking out. A 0.1 second improvement off every wall in a 200m race = 0.3–0.7 seconds total. This is where races are won and lost.
Streamline

Hands stacked (dominant on top), arms past ears, core rigid. A blade, not a V.

Kick depth

30–40cm amplitude. Hips generate — knees bend 30–40° maximum only.

Swim depth

0.5–0.6m below surface. Deeper = drag spike. Shallower = power loss.

Breakout timing

Break out before velocity drops below surface speed — usually 12–14m.

FREESTYLE FLIP TURN — Race Within a Race

▾

Fast flip turn: aggressive approach, compact somersault, two-footed plant at 0.4–0.6m depth, explosive push into tight streamline. Push-off is the fastest moment in any race — elite swimmers reach 7.5+ m/s. Every 0.1 second lost in a turn must be made up swimming, which costs disproportionately more energy. "Fast flip" is explicitly programmed in your neg-split sets.

🎯 Neg Split + Fast Flip: Swim first 50 at AeT effort, second 50 building to maximum-effort flip turn. Trains the neuromuscular pattern of attacking turns under fatigue — exactly when races are won or lost in competition.
Approach

Maintain or accelerate into the wall. NEVER decelerate in final 5m.

Flip distance

Initiate 1.2–1.5 body lengths from wall — find your exact distance in practice.

Push depth

0.4–0.6m. Too shallow = surface drag. Too deep = too far to surface.

Post-turn UW

Minimum 5m dolphin kick off every wall, even in aerobic practice sets.

BREASTSTROKE PULLOUT — Legal Advantage

▾

The breaststroke pullout is the only moment in breaststroke where the swimmer can travel faster underwater than on the surface. Legal sequence: push off → streamline glide → one underwater pull (arms sweep from overhead to hips) → one dolphin kick → breakout. The glide duration is critical: too short = wasted push energy; too long = velocity bleeds off. Adam Peaty's pullout velocity exceeds 2 m/s.

🎯 Pullout drill: Push off the wall, count your streamline glide. Begin the pull as you sense deceleration — not before, not after. 100 BR = shorter glide, 200 BR = longer glide to conserve energy early. Both are correct for their event.

Zone 1 — Recovery

RECOVERY ZONE

Lactate: <1.5 mmol/L  |  HR: Below 23 (Golf scale)  |  Effort: Easy conversation throughout, sustainable indefinitely

Pure active recovery. Blood lactate is being actively cleared, not produced. Used after hard sessions, during warm-up, between set rounds, and in cool-down. You cannot train yourself fitter in Zone 1 — only recover faster. Never rush through rest intervals or cool-down; they compound over weeks.

Zone 2 — Aerobic Threshold (AeT)

AEROBIC THRESHOLD — The Foundation

Lactate: 2.0–3.0 mmol/L  |  HR: 24–28 (Golf scale)  |  Effort: Full sentences, sustainable 2+ hours

The most important zone for all competitive swimmers. Builds mitochondrial density, oxidative enzyme capacity, cardiac output, and fat oxidation. The physiological adaptations are the foundation on which all other zones are built. Without a large AeT base, threshold and VO₂ work is inefficient. 80%+ of total training volume should be in Zone 2.

🎯 Program use: 20×50, 12×200, 8×400 — every major aerobic set in Meso I. HR cue: 26–28 Golf scale. DB fingerstick validates at 2–3 mmol. If you feel like you could go harder, you probably should not.
Zone 3 — Lactate Threshold

THRESHOLD — Race Endurance

Lactate: 3.0–5.0 mmol/L  |  HR: 27–31 (Golf scale)  |  Effort: Short 3–4 word sentences, ~60-minute sustainable max

Trains the body to produce and clear lactate simultaneously at a higher rate — the defining characteristic of elite race endurance. Threshold pace roughly corresponds to your best 20–30 minute performance. Appears first in Week 4. Using threshold before building aerobic base produces poor adaptation and slow recovery.

Zone 4 — VO₂ Max

VO₂ MAX — Oxygen Ceiling

Lactate: 5.0–8.0 mmol/L  |  HR: 31–35 (Golf scale)  |  Effort: Single words only, 4–8 minute sustainable max

Maximizes the rate of oxygen delivery and utilization. Produces acute cardiovascular stress that forces the heart to adapt its stroke volume and cardiac output. Short, very intense intervals (3–8 minutes at max aerobic power). Requires 48–72 hours between sessions. Used sparingly and only on top of a large base.

Zone 5 — Anaerobic Speed

ANAEROBIC SPEED — Pure Velocity

Lactate: 8.0+ mmol/L  |  HR: Near max  |  Effort: Unspeakable. All-out sprint effort.

Trains the PCr and glycolytic energy systems, neuromuscular firing rate, and peak swimming velocity. Sets are extremely short (10–30 seconds) with very long rest (2–5 minutes). Used in taper and sprint cycles only. Zone 5 work on top of insufficient aerobic base produces only fatigue, not meaningful adaptation.
