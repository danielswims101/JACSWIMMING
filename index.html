The Science
BUILT ON PHYSIOLOGY

Every session has a specific physiological purpose. Here's the framework that underpins this entire program.

Energy Systems

AEROBIC THRESHOLD (AeT)

Training at 2–3 mmol/L blood lactate builds the mitochondrial density, oxidative enzyme capacity, and fat oxidation ability that underlies all elite swimming performance.

Your HR cue is 26–28 on the Golf scale — beats counted over 10 seconds at the wall. Going faster produces worse aerobic adaptations and a much higher recovery cost.

  • Recovery: <1.5 mmol — active rest, clearing lactate
  • Sub-threshold: 1.5–2.5 mmol — comfortable aerobic build
  • AeT zone: 2.5–3.0 mmol — primary training target
  • Threshold: 3.0–4.5 mmol — race-pace endurance
  • VO₂ max: 4.5–8+ mmol — speed endurance work
Periodization

MESO-CYCLE STRUCTURE

Meso I (Weeks 1–4) establishes aerobic base stroke-by-stroke with progressively increasing volume. Week 4 introduces threshold for the first time. Meso II adds 25% more volume and layers threshold work on top of the now-solid aerobic foundation.

The sequence matters: you cannot shortcut the base-building phase without paying for it in adaptation quality and recovery cost later in the cycle.

  • Wk 1: Freestyle AeT base — 20×50 benchmark set
  • Wk 2: Backstroke + freestyle crossover — 5×100 BK
  • Wk 3: Breaststroke + butterfly integration
  • Wk 4: First threshold — 6×400 FR at HR 28–30
  • Meso 2: All strokes, threshold volume up 25%

ENTRY & CATCH — Where Power Starts

▾

The most important moment in freestyle happens before the pull even begins. The hand enters narrow — inside the shoulder line — at a 10–15° downward angle with zero splash. Caeleb Dressel enters at 11 and 1 o'clock and never wider. From entry, the arm extends fully forward, loading the lats before the catch begins.

The high elbow catch is the single most impactful skill in freestyle. Your elbow rises and flares outward while the wrist presses down, creating a large vertical forearm surface pressing against still water. This is fundamentally different from pressing the hand down, which just pushes you backward.

🎯 Coaching cue: "Reach around a barrel." Your elbow sets high and wide before the hand moves backward at all. The forearm is the paddle. The elbow is the fulcrum. Never let the elbow collapse inward early.
Entry angle

10–15° below horizontal. Never flat — that creates splash and wasted entry energy.

Elbow height

Elbow stays above wrist throughout the entire catch phase — no exceptions.

Catch depth

Wrist reaches vertical at 30–40 cm depth before the pull begins backward.

Elite marker

Wrist-to-forearm angle at maximum catch depth: 90° or less.

BODY ROTATION & POSITION

▾

Rotation is not a stylistic choice — it's the primary mechanism that loads the larger lat and core muscles into the pull. Sprinting uses 35–45° of rotation, distance uses 25–35°. The timing is precise: when the left hand catches, the right hip drives downward simultaneously, torquing the body and preloading the pulling arm's lat before it fires.

Head position is equally critical. The hairline sits at the surface with eyes looking at the pool floor. Even a 2-inch head lift creates significant drag by forcing the hips lower. Test yourself: if your hips are visible near the surface and your feet create a narrow wake rather than splashing sideways, your body position is correct.

🎯 Coaching cue: "Your hips drive your arms." When rotation drops under fatigue, the kick is what failed — not the arms. Fix the kick first, and rotation follows automatically.

GOLF SCORE — Your AeT Pacing Tool

▾

Golf Score = your 50m split in seconds + your stroke count per 50m. A swimmer doing a 32-second 50m with 28 strokes has a Golf score of 60. Lower is better. The goal is to find the exact combination of stroke rate and distance-per-stroke that minimizes the score, then hold that combination precisely at AeT heart rate.

In your program, "Des 1–5 to Golf BA" means descending through reps 1–5 by tuning tempo until you hit your Golf Best Average. Reps 6–10 lock that number in under sustained effort.

🎯 Elite benchmarks: Below 55 = world-class efficiency. 55–62 = senior national standard. 62–70 = strong competitive level. 70–80 = actively developing. Above 80 = beginner — major efficiency gains available.
HR target

Always aerobic only — Golf Score 26–28 Golf scale. Never threshold during Golf sets.

DPS +2 drill

Take 2 fewer strokes per length than your natural count. Forces better technique.

Recording

Log split and stroke count every single rep. Weekly trends show real progress.

Equipment

Snorkel + Paddle on Top removes breath timing, isolating pure stroke mechanics.

KICK — The Hidden 30–40% of Propulsion

▾

Elite sprinters derive 30–40% of their propulsion from the kick alone. The 6-beat kick (six kicks per complete arm cycle) is standard for sprints and middle-distance. Many elite distance swimmers use a 2-beat kick to conserve energy. The kick originates from the hip, travels through a slightly bent knee (30–40°), and ends with a flexible, plantarflexed ankle acting as a passive flipper.

A knee-driven "bicycle" kick creates drag and produces almost zero propulsion. Daily ankle plantarflexion stretching — seriously, every single day — is the single highest-return physical investment you can make for kick speed. Ankle flexibility is more limiting than strength for the vast majority of swimmers.

🎯 Coaching cue: "Kick from your hip pocket, not your knee." The foot is just a flipper following the hip. Band pull sets in your program remove the kick entirely to isolate hip drive, then reintegrate it deliberately.

BODY POSITION & THE DUCK DRILL

▾

The Duck device keeps your head in precise backstroke alignment during training. Correct position means hips at the surface, ears slightly submerged, and eyes oriented slightly toward your feet. Ryan Murphy's hips sit so high in the water that his knees barely break the surface during the kick. Any hip drop creates exponentially increasing drag — even a small drop is significant.

🎯 Coaching cue: "Squeeze a coin between your glutes and point your belly button at the ceiling." Hip drop is almost always a core stability or body-awareness issue, not a flexibility problem. The Duck tool provides real-time feedback that no mirror or video can replicate.
Head position

No chin tuck, no looking at feet. One ear-depth below the surface. Completely neutral neck.

Kick plane

Feet barely break the surface. Knee-driven backstroke kick creates immediate drag.

Shoulder roll

45–55° rotation per stroke. More rotation = more lat engagement = more power output.

Stability test

Pick a ceiling tile and keep it in frame throughout the entire length without movement.

CATCH & PULL — Pinky Entry & 2-2-2 Drill

▾

Backstroke entry is pinky-first, with the hand rotated so the thumb points inward toward the body centerline. The arm enters directly above the shoulder — crossing the centerline creates a snake-body wiggle, while entering too wide wastes rotation energy. After entry, extend fully past your head and feel the lats engage before the catch begins.

The 2-2-2 drill from your program — two right-arm strokes, two left-arm strokes, two full strokes — isolates each side of the catch without the pressure of maintaining tempo. Listen for the difference in sound between a clean entry and a noisy one. Noise means the hand is entering at the wrong angle.

🎯 The S-curve pull path is more pronounced in backstroke than freestyle: press outward, sweep inward, then push back. All three phases connect without pause. Thumb exits at the hip as the elbow bends on recovery.

THE 2K1 SCULL — Your Program's Core Drill

▾

2k1 (two kicks, one stroke) is the foundational breaststroke drill in this program. By doubling the kick before each pull, it slows tempo dramatically — forcing the swimmer to feel the glide, the catch, and every phase of the out-scull before pulling. Qin Haiyang uses an extraordinarily patient out-scull before initiating the catch, and 2k1 is how you develop that same patience.

🎯 Program progression: Deep 2k1 scull → Shallow 2k1 scull → Flat rhythm → Full speed. Each phase decreases scull depth, building from exaggerated feel to race-speed feel within a single training block.
Scull path

Hands press OUT first, then IN, then back — a full heart-shaped pattern every cycle.

Deep version

Hands dip below chest level — exaggerates the feeling of water pressure on the palm.

Flat version

Hands at surface plane — matches actual race-speed technique timing exactly.

Common error

Collapsing elbows inward before the out-scull is complete — kills propulsion immediately.

KICK — 60–70% of Breaststroke Propulsion

▾

Breaststroke kick generates 60–70% of all propulsion, making it the most important physical skill in the stroke. The heels draw up narrow — not wide — because a wide recovery creates a massive drag spike that cancels much of the kick benefit. Feet flex outward (dorsiflexion + eversion) into "duck feet" position before sweeping in a circular pattern and finishing together in streamline.

The most common flaw is an asymmetric or bicycle-style kick where one foot sweeps differently from the other. Your 10×100 BR kick descend sets directly target the specific endurance needed to maintain correct kick mechanics through the critical final 50m of a 200 BR.

🎯 Coaching cue: "Heels narrow, toes wide, sweep out-around-and-together." The full streamline extension after the kick — both hands and both feet aligned — is not optional. It is where breaststroke speed comes from.

TIMING — Pull, Breathe, Kick, Glide (In That Order)

▾

The pull and kick must never overlap — this is the most important rule in breaststroke. The rigid sequence is: pull → breathe → kick → glide. Any overlap creates opposing forces that directly cancel propulsion. The breath occurs during the in-sweep as hands come inward and elbows tuck — the head rides the natural body lift from the pull rather than actively popping up.

Adam Peaty's head barely clears the surface even at sprint 100m pace because he allows the stroke mechanics to produce the lift rather than forcing it with neck flexion. This keeps drag minimal at the most critical moment of each cycle.

🎯 Build-each-25 rule from your program: effort level +1, +2, +3, +4 across a 100m. Each 25 increases tempo — training timing precision under race-speed fatigue, which is exactly where 200 BR races are won or lost.

UNDULATION — The Wave From the Ground Up

▾

Butterfly is fundamentally driven by undulation, not by the arms. The movement initiates when the chest presses down — this causes the hips to rise, which causes the feet to drive downward in the kick. It travels as a wave from chest through hips to feet. Michael Phelps' butterfly is described as dolphin-like because his undulation starts at the chest and propagates through with zero interruption or stiffness.

The 2-2-2 drill in your program breaks this down: two right-arm strokes, two left-arm strokes, two full butterfly strokes. In drill mode, one dolphin kick per arm stroke. This isolates the one-arm pull pattern and makes bilateral asymmetries obvious before they become ingrained problems.

🎯 Coaching cue: "Chest DOWN first — everything else follows." Hip amplitude should be 20–30 cm. Less than that and you have no propulsion. More than that and you're creating massive drag on the up-phase.
Kick 1 timing

First kick fires at hand entry and extension — loading the body for the pull.

Kick 2 timing

Second kick fires at the push/exit phase — accelerating the body forward.

OA fly drill

One-arm butterfly: free arm provides balance while working arm isolates the catch.

Wiff tool

No-splash entry drill — forces 45° downward hand angle with zero surface disturbance.

UNDERWATER DOLPHIN KICK — The 5th Stroke

▾

Underwater dolphin kick is the fastest way a human moves through water. At elite level, swimmers hold higher velocity underwater than they can sustain on the surface. Your program features UW 15m on nearly every single session — 15 meters is the legal maximum from every wall. Caeleb Dressel's underwater velocity off the start and turns actually exceeds his surface sprint pace.

The kick originates at the hips, traveling as a wave through the body to the feet. Knees bend only 30–40° on the downbeat. Swimming depth is critical: 0.5–0.6m below the surface. Deeper causes drag. Shallower bleeds power. Breakout timing is equally important — you must surface before your velocity drops below your surface swimming speed, typically at 12–14m.

🎯 Program drill: "UW 15 IM Drill @ 1:00" — every single rep, maximize depth hold, streamline tightness, and kick tempo before surfacing. A 0.1-second improvement off every wall in a 200m race is worth 0.3–0.7 seconds in total race time. This is where races are decided.
Streamline

Hands stacked dominant-on-top, arms pressed past ears, core completely rigid. A blade.

Kick depth

25–35 cm amplitude. Hip-driven. Knees only 30–40° maximum bend on downbeat.

Swim depth

0.5–0.6m below surface. Test: your streamline is invisible from the pool deck.

Elite rate

180–210 kicks/min. Your program's fin kick sets build toward this ceiling progressively.

FREESTYLE FLIP TURN — A Race Within the Race

▾

The turn is the fastest moment in any race. Elite swimmers reach push-off velocities of 7.5+ m/s — speeds impossible to achieve swimming. Every 0.1 second lost in a turn must be recouped through swimming at a much higher metabolic cost. Your neg-split sets explicitly program "fast flip" practice because turn speed under fatigue is a distinct, trainable skill.

🎯 Key rule: Never decelerate in the final 5 meters before the wall. Approach at full speed, initiate the flip 1.2–1.5 body lengths out, plant both feet at 0.4–0.6m depth, and drive into tight streamline with minimum 5m UW dolphin kick off every wall.
Flip initiation

1.2–1.5 body lengths from the wall. Find your exact personal distance in practice.

Push depth

0.4–0.6m. Shallower = surface drag immediately. Deeper = too far to return to surface.

Post-turn UW

Minimum 5m dolphin kick off every single wall — even in aerobic practice sets.

Elite speed

7.5+ m/s push-off velocity. This is the fastest you ever move in any race.

BREASTSTROKE PULLOUT — The Legal Advantage

▾

The breaststroke pullout is the only point in the stroke where you travel faster underwater than on the surface. The legal sequence is: push off → streamline glide → one full underwater pull (arms sweep from overhead all the way to the hips) → one dolphin kick → breakout. Adam Peaty's pullout velocity exceeds 2 m/s.

The glide duration is the key variable. Start the pull the moment you sense velocity beginning to drop — not before (wasted push energy) and not after (you've already slowed significantly). For 100 BR this is a shorter glide than for 200 BR, where conserving energy from each turn matters across eight walls.

Injury Prevention

COMMON ISSUES & FIXES

  • Swimmer's shoulder: serratus weakness — serratus punches daily without exception
  • Breaststroker's knee: hip adductor imbalance — Copenhagen planks 3× per week
  • Low back pain: anterior pelvic tilt — daily 90/90 hip flexor mobility work
  • Bicep tendon strain: band pull overload — progress pull volume gradually
  • Ankle impingement: insufficient plantarflexion ROM — daily loading required
  • Neck pain in backstroke: Duck drill imbalance — daily neck CARs and posture work

Zone 1 — Recovery

RECOVERY ZONE

Lactate: <1.5 mmol/L  ·  HR: Below 23 Golf scale  ·  Effort: Effortless conversation, sustainable indefinitely

Pure active recovery. Blood lactate is being cleared faster than it's being produced — this is physiological rest, not training. Used during warm-up, between high-intensity set rounds, and in cool-down. You cannot improve your fitness in Zone 1. You can only help yourself recover faster for the next session. Never rush through cool-down; lactate clearance here compounds over weeks.

Zone 2 — Aerobic Threshold (AeT)

AEROBIC THRESHOLD — The True Foundation

Lactate: 2.0–3.0 mmol/L  ·  HR: 24–28 Golf scale  ·  Effort: Full conversation, sustainable for 2+ hours

The most important training zone in all of swimming. Zone 2 builds mitochondrial density, oxidative enzyme capacity, cardiac output, and the fat-oxidation efficiency that underlies every other zone's performance. The physiological adaptations from this zone form the base on which threshold and VO₂ work are built. Without a large, well-developed aerobic base, higher-intensity work produces mediocre adaptation at high recovery cost.

80%+ of total training volume in this program is Zone 2. If you feel like you could go harder during these sets — you probably should not.

🎯 Program use: 20×50 benchmark, 12×200 Meso 2, 8×400 descend-hold — every major aerobic set in Meso I. Validate with HR 26–28 Golf scale and DB fingerstick at 2–3 mmol.
Zone 3 — Lactate Threshold

THRESHOLD — Race Endurance

Lactate: 3.0–5.0 mmol/L  ·  HR: 27–31 Golf scale  ·  Effort: 3–4 word sentences maximum, ~60-minute sustainable limit

Threshold training makes the body more efficient at producing and clearing lactate simultaneously at a higher rate — the defining metabolic characteristic of elite race endurance. Your threshold pace roughly corresponds to your best possible 20–30 minute effort. Zone 3 appears first in Week 4 and expands progressively through Meso II. Using threshold before the aerobic base is established produces poor adaptation and disproportionately slow recovery.

Zone 4 — VO₂ Max

VO₂ MAX — Oxygen Ceiling

Lactate: 5.0–8.0 mmol/L  ·  HR: 31–35 Golf scale  ·  Effort: Single words only, 4–8 minute sustainable limit

Zone 4 maximizes the rate of oxygen delivery and utilization. The cardiovascular stress forces adaptation in cardiac stroke volume and output — the physical size and efficiency of the heart's pumping capacity. Intervals are short (3–8 minutes at maximum aerobic power) and recovery cost is high: 48–72 hours between sessions. Used sparingly and only after a substantial base and threshold foundation is established.

Zone 5 — Anaerobic / Speed

ANAEROBIC SPEED — Pure Velocity

Lactate: 8.0+ mmol/L  ·  HR: Near maximum  ·  Effort: Completely unspeakable. All-out.

Zone 5 trains the phosphocreatine and glycolytic energy systems, peak neuromuscular firing rate, and maximum swimming velocity. Sets are extremely short — 10 to 30 seconds — with very long rest periods of 2–5 minutes to allow near-complete recovery between reps. Used during taper periods and sprint training cycles. Zone 5 work on top of an insufficient aerobic base produces only fatigue, not meaningful adaptation.
